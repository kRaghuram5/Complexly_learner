The Complete Visual Guide to Complex Numbers

Origins: Solving the Impossible

For centuries, numbers lived comfortably on the real number line. But in the 16th century, Italian mathematician Gerolamo Cardano stumbled upon a mystery while solving cubic equations: certain solutions required taking the square root of a negative number.

The equation x² + 1 = 0 has no real solution because no real number squared equals –1.

Rather than discard the problem, mathematicians imagined a new number: i, the imaginary unit, defined by:

i² = –1

Over time, pioneers like Leonhard Euler in the 18th century formalized this concept, leading to the birth of complex numbers — a fusion of real and imaginary numbers.

Definition: What is a Complex Number?

A complex number has the form:

z = a + bi
  • a → Real part (Re(z))
  • b → Imaginary part (Im(z))
  • i → Imaginary unit (i² = –1)

Think of a as your East–West coordinate, and b as your North–South coordinate. Together, they locate your complex number in a 2D space.

Visualizing: The Complex Plane

Instead of a single number line, complex numbers live on a complex plane:

  • The horizontal axis represents the real part.
  • The vertical axis represents the imaginary part.

The point (a, b) can also be drawn as a vector from the origin — a key idea for the next section.

Modulus and Argument

A complex number as a vector has two key measurements:

  • Modulus (r) → the vector’s length
  • Argument (θ) → the vector’s angle with the positive real axis
r = |z| = √(a² + b²) θ = arg(z) = tan⁻¹(b / a)

From Rectangular to Polar Form

Using trigonometry:

a = r cos θ b = r sin θ z = r (cos θ + i sin θ)

This polar form makes multiplication, division, and exponentiation much simpler. In polar form, multiplying two complex numbers means multiplying their moduli and adding their arguments.

Why Complex Numbers Matter

Complex numbers are not just a theoretical invention — they are essential in:

  • Electrical engineering (AC circuits)
  • Signal processing
  • Quantum mechanics
  • Computer graphics and 3D transformations

They bridge algebra and geometry, giving us a complete system where every polynomial equation has a solution.

All of this started with the simple yet radical thought: “What if a number could have a square equal to –1?”
